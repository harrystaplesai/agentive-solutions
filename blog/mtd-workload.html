MTD for Income Tax: What It Actually Means for Your Practice’s Workload

Harry Staples · 9 min read

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax has been delayed so many times that a lot of practices stopped thinking about it seriously. That stops being a reasonable position from April 2026.

From 6 April 2026, sole traders and landlords with combined gross income above £50,000 move into the new regime. Annual Self Assessment returns get replaced with quarterly digital submissions plus a year-end final declaration. The threshold drops to £30,000 in April 2027 and £20,000 in April 2028. With each wave, more of your clients come in scope, and more of your team’s time goes on managing the new rhythm.

This article is about what that actually means for a small or mid-sized practice in practical terms, because most of the coverage focuses on what clients need to do. The impact on the people running the practice tends to get less attention.

The submission maths are sobering

Under the old model, a sole trader client meant one Self Assessment return per year. Under MTD, the same client means four quarterly updates plus an End of Period Statement plus a Final Declaration. That’s six touchpoints per year per affected client, compared to one.

As one analysis put it, the traditional one return per year model is being replaced by a five submission per client model. A practice managing 200 clients moving into MTD in 2026 goes from filing 200 returns to managing 1,000 digital touchpoints annually, plus year-end work on top of that.

The quarterly updates themselves are relatively simple. They’re cumulative year-to-date income and expense summaries, and HMRC has confirmed that tax and accounting adjustments can wait until the year-end final declaration. But simple doesn’t mean effortless. Someone still has to chase the client for their records. Someone still has to check the data makes sense. Someone still has to file on time. Four times a year.

The chasing problem gets four times worse

If you’re already spending significant time chasing clients for their annual records, quarterly submissions multiply that problem directly.

Karbon’s data found that practices using automation to chase clients were saving 3.2 hours per employee per week. That figure comes from the annual cycle. Under MTD, the chasing happens four times as often for every affected client. The practices that haven’t built systems for this are going to feel it.

ICAEW’s Senior Technical Manager for Tax Policy, Lindsey Wicks, made the point clearly when MTD was confirmed for 2026. Getting clients ready creates additional administrative and time costs for accountants, many of whom will struggle to pass those costs on in full to their clients. The billing model, the staffing model, and the client communication model all need thinking through before the quarterly rhythm starts.

Clients need more hand-holding than you might expect

MTD requires clients to maintain digital records throughout the year. For a landlord who has been writing income and expenses in a notebook since 2003, that’s a significant behaviour change. For a sole trader using a spreadsheet who has never touched accounting software, that’s training, setup support, and ongoing questions every quarter.

ICAEW’s Practice Assurance Monitoring data already showed that many practices were stretched on compliance basics before MTD arrived. The new regime adds client education and software onboarding to an already loaded team.

This is the part that tends to get overlooked in conversations about MTD readiness. The software choice and the filing mechanics are manageable. The volume of client support conversations that come with transitioning a client base to quarterly digital reporting is the harder problem.

What the better-prepared practices are doing

The practices I see handling this well have done two things. They’ve got ahead on client segmentation – working out who comes into scope in 2026, who follows in 2027, and what each group needs from them before April. And they’ve built or bought systems that handle the routine communication side automatically.

The chase for quarterly records, the reminder that a submission window is open, the confirmation when something has been received – these are tasks that consume hours of team time every week and that an AI employee can handle without any human involvement. The partners then focus on the actual accounting work, which is where their value and their margin sit.

The practices that treat MTD as purely a compliance challenge are going to find 2026 difficult. The ones that treat it as a reason to finally sort out their client communication infrastructure are going to come out of it stronger.

A practical note on timing

The first quarterly update for 2026/27 is due by 7 August 2026, covering the period from 6 April to 5 July. That gives practices a few months after April to get the new rhythm established, but HMRC has confirmed that for clients entering MTD in 2026, there’s a soft landing period where late submission penalty points don’t apply for the first four quarterly updates. That grace period doesn’t extend to clients entering in 2027.

April 2026 is the starting gun, not the deadline.
