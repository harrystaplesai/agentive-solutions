The Real Cost of Chasing Clients for Documents

Harry Staples · 7 min read

Every accounting practice has a version of the same Tuesday morning. The partner arrives, opens their inbox, and the first twenty minutes of the day go on chasing the same clients for the same documents they asked for two weeks ago. Bank statements. Payroll records. Receipts that somehow never materialise until the deadline is close enough to feel dangerous.

It’s one of those problems that’s been around so long that most practices have stopped thinking of it as a problem. It’s just part of the job.

The cost of treating it that way is higher than it looks.

What the chasing actually costs

Karbon tracked this across their user base and found that practices using automation to chase clients were saving 3.2 hours per employee per week. That’s one full working day every fortnight spent on follow-up communication that generates no billable value whatsoever.

For a partner billing at £150 an hour, three hours a week of document chasing is £450 worth of time. Every week. That’s over £23,000 a year in opportunity cost from a single fee earner.

Across a team of four or five people – including junior staff whose time is less expensive but whose hours are still finite – the aggregate number gets uncomfortable quickly. And that’s before you account for the psychological cost of interruption. Research consistently shows that it takes around 25 minutes to regain deep focus after a disruption. Document chasing doesn’t just eat the minutes directly. It fragments the hours around them.

Why clients are slow to send things

It’s worth being honest about this rather than just treating late clients as a nuisance.

Most clients are running businesses. The bank statement their accountant needs is genuinely low on their priority list compared to the customer they need to invoice, the supplier they’re chasing, and the staff issue that came up on Monday. They intend to send it. They forget. They get an email reminder and open it on their phone at 7pm, think “I’ll do it at my desk tomorrow,” and then don’t.

This isn’t a character flaw. It’s how people operate when they’re busy. The solution is a system that makes it easy for them to respond and persistent enough to keep the request visible without requiring a human to manually chase it every three days.

Phone calls work better than emails for getting responses, as AccountingWEB members have noted repeatedly over the years. But phone calls from a partner or senior member of staff are expensive. They also feel disproportionate for a routine document request. Nobody wants to field a call from their accountant about a bank statement they’ve been meaning to forward.

The right medium is a WhatsApp or SMS message that feels personal, arrives at a sensible time, and follows up automatically until the document arrives. That’s what most practices are trying to replicate with manual chasing, and what takes hours of human time every week.

The MTD multiplier

Under Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, which comes into force for clients above £50,000 income from April 2026, quarterly submissions replace the annual return. Clients who previously sent their records once a year now need to provide information four times a year.

For a practice that already struggles with document chasing in the annual cycle, a quarterly cycle is a significant increase in pressure. The 3.2 hours a week that Karbon’s data identified was based on the old model. Quarterly reporting multiplies the frequency of every chase by four for every affected client.

The practices that have already built systems to handle document chasing automatically are going to absorb this transition without much disruption. The ones still doing it manually are going to feel it acutely.

What good looks like

The best approach I’ve seen treats document chasing as an automated process from the moment a job is opened. The client gets a WhatsApp message explaining what’s needed and why. If they haven’t responded in 48 hours, they get a follow-up. If they still haven’t responded after a week, someone on the team gets a flag. The human only gets involved at the escalation stage, not at the initial and follow-up stages.

This isn’t complicated. It’s just a process that most practices haven’t built because the problem crept in gradually and got normalised.

The test is straightforward. If a senior person in your practice is spending time on routine document chase messages, that’s time that could be spent on something that actually requires their expertise. The question worth asking is how much of any given week that describes.
