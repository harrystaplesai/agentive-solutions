Why Accounting Practices Lose New Clients Before They Even Know About It

Harry Staples · 8 min read

There’s a version of this that happens every week at small accounting practices across the UK. Someone finds you through Google, or a referral, or your website. They fill in your contact form or call your number. They’re ready to switch accountants, or they’re a new business looking for their first one. The timing is right.

Then nothing happens for a day or two.

By the time someone gets back to them, they’ve already spoken to two other practices and one of them has already sent an engagement letter.

You never knew about the lead. It came in, sat unanswered, and walked straight out the door.

The scale of the problem is worse than most partners think

Data from Moneypenny, working with Insight 6, found that nearly half of UK accounting practices fail to respond to enquiries within two days. Research from Paperclip found that 47% of small business calls go unanswered, and 85% of people who don’t get through never try again. They just call someone else.

At an average client value of around £3,000 a year, a practice missing even a handful of enquiries each month is leaving tens of thousands of pounds on the table annually. The maths is straightforward even if the problem often goes unnoticed because the lost leads leave no trace. They don’t complain. They don’t leave reviews. They just disappear.

Why it happens

It’s not negligence. Most practices I’ve worked with care deeply about client service. The issue is structural.

Partners and senior staff are the people most capable of handling a new enquiry conversation. They’re also the people buried in client work, deadlines and compliance. When a new enquiry comes in at 5:30pm, or on a Sunday evening, or during the January Self Assessment crunch, there’s nobody with the time, energy or mandate to respond immediately.

The person who does have capacity is often a junior admin team member who doesn’t feel confident qualifying a prospect or quoting fees. So the enquiry gets noted, passed along, and somewhere in that handoff it loses momentum.

MTD for Income Tax makes this worse. From April 2026, practices managing clients above the £50,000 income threshold face quarterly digital submissions on top of everything else. That’s a 4x increase in client touchpoints with no corresponding increase in headcount. The team is stretched further. The phone rings. Nobody picks up.

What the fastest-growing practices do differently

The practices that consistently convert new enquiries into clients have one thing in common. They’ve taken the first response out of the hands of their team entirely.

Some use dedicated phone answering services. Some use chat tools on their websites. The more forward-thinking ones are using AI to handle the initial response, qualify the prospect, and get a booking into the diary without anyone on the team having to lift a finger.

The response goes out within seconds. The prospect gets a professional, warm interaction. The partner gets a summary of the enquiry and a meeting already booked.

The gap between responding in 30 seconds and responding in 30 hours is the gap between winning and losing that client. Most people making an enquiry are talking to more than one practice at the same time.

This isn’t about being bigger or better resourced

I want to be clear about something. The practices winning on enquiry response speed are overwhelmingly small practices with lean teams. They’ve just stopped trying to do it manually.

A ten-person practice that responds to every enquiry within a minute, any time of day, any day of the week, is going to consistently outperform a thirty-person practice that replies on Tuesday morning when someone gets round to it.

Size has very little to do with it. Process does.

The question worth asking is simple. When a new enquiry came in last Sunday evening, what actually happened to it?
